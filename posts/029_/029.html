Superagency Review #5: I’m Somewhat Convinced (Final Part)

Chapters 9-11: I really resonated with the conclusion!

The final section of Superagency (pages 185-end) surprised me. After spending most of the book skeptical of Hoffman’s optimism, I found myself… somewhat convinced.

Not about everything. But enough to shift from reflexive skepticism to cautious possibility.

Chapter 9: Wait, Now Regulation Is Good?

The book opens Chapter 9 with self-driving cars and network effects - the more people use cars, the more valuable roads become.

Then Hoffman pivots: “a web of regulations fueled extraordinary increases to personal freedom.”

This felt like flip-flopping. Earlier chapters emphasized permissionless innovation and limited government. Now regulation is the hero?

But I think I was wrong to see it as contradiction. Hoffman is arguing for nuance. Not “regulation bad” or “regulation good.” Rather: the right regulation at the right time enables innovation.

The “holy trinity”: innovation, broad deployment, and evidence-based regulation. Not innovation alone.

I appreciate this more than the earlier black-and-white framing.

Technology and Freedom

Hoffman frames freedom as a balancing act. The ability to drive 2,000 miles across state lines isn’t enumerated in the Constitution, but it’s an “extraordinary freedom we enjoy thanks to tech innovation and regulation.”

He describes modern America as “an endless odyssey of low-key administrative tyranny and casual surveillance” - the web of different state laws controlling how you move, big tech brokering your data at every touchpoint.

Yet that same infrastructure makes interstate travel safe and lawful.

This philosophical take on technology and freedom is what the book does best. We interact with tech daily but rarely think about the rights it gives and takes away.

The question: If cars inspired waves of regulation, AI will too. Is Hoffman flip-flopping or being realistic?

Mill and Networked Autonomy

Hoffman invokes John Stuart Mill: individual freedom matters not just for itself but to contribute to society’s overall well-being. Operating individually, parts are strong. Operating together, they become stronger.

He calls this “networked autonomy.”

I’m skeptical of Hoffman’s reading of Mill. Mill’s utilitarianism relies on the assumption that self-interest isn’t innate - that humans can be educated to prioritize collective well-being over individual interests.

But evolutionary psychology suggests self-interest is deeply ingrained. While humans cooperate, it often has self-interested motivations: reciprocity, reputation-building, survival.

Hoffman uses Mill to argue that large-scale public works (roads, GPS, AI) help us “live the best version of ourselves.” But does that generalize to AI? The book doesn’t explain how AI fits this story or what the specific risks are.

Chapter 10: Are Technologies Really Liberating?

Hoffman claims technologies “often depicted by their critics as dehumanizing and constraining generally turn out to be humanizing and liberating.”

I want counter-examples. What about technologies that were constraining?

  • Social media promised connection, delivered addiction and polarization
  • Gig economy platforms promised flexibility, created precarious labor
  • Surveillance tech promised security, enabled authoritarianism

The book mentions AI preserving national values (Singapore, France) but doesn’t ask: Do we need AI that preserves national values? What if those values conflict?

Hoffman invokes Harari’s “imagined order” - the myths modern states use to enable cooperation. But myths can also justify control.

Chapter 11: You Can Get There From Here

I loved this conclusion.

Hoffman argues that to reap AI’s benefits, we need an “exploratory, adaptive, forward-looking mindset.” Only by asking what CAN we do with AI - not just what will go wrong - can we imagine better possibilities.

Iterative deployment, testing through societal use, makes technology more humanistic. As imperfect as it is, AI excites me.

This resonates deeply. I was born in an era where nothing felt as forward-pushing as AI. As a digital native, it really does feel like we’re living at the frontier of something new, pushing boundaries of what’s possible.

And at the end of the day, I think that’s beautiful.

What Actually Convinced Me

Not Hoffman’s optimism about American exceptionalism. Not the claims about democratization. Not the argument that innovation always distributes benefits fairly.

What convinced me: The book’s willingness to hold contradictions.

  • Permissionless innovation and evidence-based regulation
  • Individual autonomy and networked coordination
  • Rapid deployment and safety concerns
  • Optimism and acknowledgment of risks

The world isn’t black-and-white. AI won’t be purely liberating or purely constraining. The question is whether we can guide it toward the better outcomes.

Hoffman thinks we can. I’m not sure. But I’m less certain of my skepticism.

The Question That Remains

Throughout the book, I kept asking: Who benefits?

GPS created $1.4 trillion in value - who captured it? Roads enabled freedom - who could afford cars? AI will democratize access - for whom?

Hoffman focuses on aggregate benefit. I focus on distribution.

The book hasn’t convinced me that innovation distributes fairly without intentional policy forcing it. But it’s convinced me that refusing innovation because distribution is imperfect means giving up on solving hard problems.

That’s the tension I’m left with.

Where I Landed

I started this book as a skeptic. I end it as… a cautious optimist?

Hoffman’s vision is compelling: AI as infrastructure, like roads or GPS. Public benefit through smart regulation. American innovation as a force for democratic values.

But the distributional concerns remain. The risks of surveillance, control, and concentrated power are real.

What changed: I’m no longer reflexively skeptical. I see the optimistic case. I understand why people believe in AI’s potential to be genuinely liberating.

What didn’t change: I still think who benefits matters as much as whether benefits exist.

The book made me think harder about AI than I expected. For a “gloomer,” that’s worth something.

As imperfect as AI is, and as skeptical as I remain about who profits, I can’t deny: we’re at the frontier of something new. And that’s exciting.
