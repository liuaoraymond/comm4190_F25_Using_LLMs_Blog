Superagency Review #3: Testing, Benchmarking, and the Gamification of Regulation

A skeptical reading of Hoffman’s testing-first vision for AI governance

Chapter 5: Testing, Testing, 1, 2 (Infinite)

This entry covers pages 99 through roughly 143 of Superagency. Hoffman and Beato shift from high-level optimism to the mechanics of how AI progress supposedly happens: testing, benchmarking, competitive iteration, and a self-regulatory culture that resembles a Cold War-era innovation race.

What stood out is not just how the authors frame testing but why: as the core engine of American technological leadership. The chapter leans heavily on a familiar narrative: American dynamism versus Chinese scale; democracy versus state-led efficiency; permissionless innovation versus precautionary restraint.

I found parts of this more convincing than previous chapters, while others raised the same anxieties lingering since Chapter 1.

Media framing of AI competition often echoes Cold War rhetoric

The Cold War Parallel

The authors highlight the ubiquity of phrases like “AI arms race” and “AI Space Race.” Hoffman treats these terms as framing devices that motivate competition and accelerate innovation.

But the Cold War metaphor brings baggage: - national survival stakes - winner-take-all framing - justification for speed over caution - a belief that being second is equivalent to losing

Competitive framing can motivate innovation, but it can also suppress dissent, crowd out critical voices, and pressure policymakers into reactionary thinking.

Democracy as Competitive Advantage - Or Ideological Comfort?

Hoffman argues that the United States maintains an edge because democratic systems encourage testing, openness, and iterative trial-and-error.

But this raises uncomfortable questions: Is American-style innovation the only acceptable hegemony? What is inherently wrong with China being technologically ahead?

The book frames America as working from an “innocent until proven guilty” innovation model, and China as “guilty until proven safe.” Whether either extreme works for frontier AI is an open question.

Benchmarks as Pseudo-Regulation

One key argument: benchmarks can function as non-binding substitutes for regulation.

Hoffman writes: “Testing elevates the focus from compliance to continuous improvement. It is regulation, gamified.”

But gamifying regulation produces issues: - optimizing for scores, not safety - metrics used as PR - cherry-picking performance tests - ignoring bad actors entirely

Real regulation exists because competition alone cannot guarantee safety.

Why Is AI Expected to Be Perfect?

The authors critique society’s near-zero error tolerance for AI.

But AI failures scale differently: - individual human errors affect one person - AI deployed at scale can replicate failures instantly - decisions are opaque and hard to interrogate

The expectation for reliability is not about perfection. It is about systemic risk management.

Innovation Is Safety - Or So the Argument Goes

The chapter suggests that innovation itself is a form of safety because faster iteration surfaces problems earlier.

But if real users become test subjects without consent, iterative deployment becomes mass experimentation.

The Ford assembly line example illustrates this tension: yes, it democratized mobility; yes, it also reshaped society in ways whose costs we now understand only decades later.

Vorsorgeprinzip and Burden of Proof

The Vorsorgeprinzip (forecaring principle) assigns the burden of proof to developers.

Hoffman sees it as anti-innovation, but in high-stakes, opaque systems, this principle may be necessary. If AI is powerful and unpredictable, why should the public bear the burden of proof?

Big Knowledge and Collective Intelligence

The chapter ends with “big knowledge” systems like Google Maps, highlighting how public infrastructure unlocks private innovation.

But dependency introduces trade-offs: - overreliance on opaque routing systems - erosion of local knowledge - vulnerabilities in critical digital infrastructure

Crowdsourced systems create powerful benefits and deep dependencies

My Running Notes

  • Who regulates the testers?
  • Who pays the price if iterative deployment harms people?
  • Is innovation inherently democratic, or is this idealized?
  • Why must speed be treated as a virtue when consequences scale exponentially?

Final Thoughts

Chapter 5 makes a strong case for testing as a driver of innovation. But it underestimates the risk of treating real people as unwitting beta testers.

Without guardrails, iterative deployment becomes mass experimentation.

There is a fine line between innovation and recklessness. The book sometimes blurs that line more than it clarifies it.
